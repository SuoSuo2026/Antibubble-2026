Antibubbles are the inverse counterpart of ordinary soap bubbles: a micrometer-thick gas shell encapsulates a millimeter-scale liquid droplet suspended within a continuous liquid phase, stabilized by surfactants whose hydrophobic tails extend into the gas film [1,2]. First reported in 1932 [3] and named in 1974 [4], antibubbles gained systematic attention after Dorbolo et al. characterized their formation, lifetime, and rupture using high-speed cinematography in 2003 [5]. Subsequent work explored antibubble acoustics [6,7], stability [8,9], and active magnetic control [10]. Crucially, the antibubble lifetime scales inversely with gravitational acceleration [11,12] — reaching ~100 s under normal gravity but becoming virtually unlimited in microgravity — a property that motivates both fundamental fluid physics and space-system applications where uncontrolled antibubble accumulation could interfere with propellant management.

The core-shell architecture of antibubbles — an independent liquid core separated from the external liquid by a thin gas film — has attracted biomedical interest. The gas film provides strong acoustic impedance contrast for harmonic ultrasound imaging with richer spectral signatures than conventional microbubbles [6,13]. The liquid core can be loaded with therapeutics and magnetically guided [10] or triggered for ultrasound-mediated release in vivo [14], positioning antibubbles as a potential fourth-generation contrast agent and targeted delivery platform.

Despite these advances, a critical bottleneck persists: no method can generate millimeter-scale antibubbles with both high efficiency and sufficient repeatability for systematic study. Traditional approaches rely on horizontal liquid interfaces. Droplet impact [15] and drop-pair [16] methods require high Weber numbers (We~10^2) and achieve yields of only ~20%, limited by rapid air-film drainage. The liquid-film method [17], where droplets pass through a suspended soap film, similarly yields ~20% at We~40 owing to film rupture when kinetic energy exceeds the film's surface energy capacity. Ultrasound-based generation [18] produces only transient antibubbles (<100 ms), while Pickering [8] and double-emulsion [14] methods yield stable but micrometer-scale antibubbles unsuitable for macroscopic transport studies.

A fundamentally different approach was introduced by Bai et al. [19], who reported that droplets injected onto a foam layer naturally channel into Plateau borders (PBs) — the liquid-filled junctions where three soap films meet. The concave PB geometry pre-entrains an air pocket, and the droplet slides along inclined film interfaces rather than impacting horizontally. This "foam method" achieves yields approaching 80% at modest Weber numbers (We~5-10). However, Bai et al. stopped at cinematographic description: the foam layer is inherently random, the PB position cannot be reliably controlled, and the physical criteria for successful multi-layer formation remain unknown.

In this Letter, we overcome both the repeatability and theoretical gaps of the foam method. We replace the random foam layer with a 3D-printed triangular-prism frame that fixes a single, well-defined PB in space [20,21], transforming a stochastic foam phenomenon into a reproducible physical system. By enclosing the setup in a vacuum chamber, we systematically vary droplet radius R, impact velocity v0, and ambient pressure P across 199 experiments. High-speed imaging reveals four universal stages of droplet-PB interaction and three outcomes: successful multi-layer formation (packing), outer coalescence, and inner coalescence. From energy conservation and gas-film lubrication theory, we derive two dimensionless criteria — a We-Bo relation and a characteristic-time ratio t/t — that together delineate the parameter space for antibubble formation and explain why the inclined PB geometry is fundamentally more efficient than horizontal-interface methods.
